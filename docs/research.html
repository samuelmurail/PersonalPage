Protein-Peptide Interactions

Proteins play a key role in almost all cellular functions. These macromolecules rarely act in isolation, but within complex interaction networks. Recent methods of characterizing protein-protein interactions (PPI) have made it possible to obtain high-throughput data on these interaction networks. These protein-protein interactions act usually via large interfaces (1500-3000 Ã…2) with no well-defined binding pocket. The modulation of these PPIs has many therapeutic applications, but the physical characteristics of these interfaces make the rational design of inhibitors difficult1. The use of peptide ligands mimicking a portion of the interaction surfaces overcomes these difficulties. These molecules are also used in a natural way, indeed peptide-protein interactions represent 15 to 40% of all cellular interactions2. These polymers have a high potential for therapeutic applications, so it is not surprising that many numerical tools have been developed to predict the structure of peptide-protein complexes.

To tackle the challenges of peptide-protein interactions we use extensive molecular dynamic (MD) simulation of multiple peptide fragments with target proteins, and thus propose a breakthrough to overcome the major limitations of current approaches. By using high precision physics-based approach, we intend to (i) identify peptide binding sites and especially key hotspots (ii) characterize hot spots peptide sequence specificity (iii) fold a target peptide in the best ranked binding sites and ultimately (iv) optimize the peptide sequence or predict de novo optimal peptide composition.

Synthetic Channels

A collaboration with a teams of chemists, the team of Mihail Barboiu, specialized in the design of materials mimicking the membrane functions (European Institute of Membranes, Montpellier). Inspired by the aquaporin and proton channel M2 of the influenza A virus, our collaborators synthesized a molecule to allow the selective transport of water molecules through the membrane. In the long term the aim of this project is to develop synthetic molecules for the purification and desalinization of water at lower cost.

Phd Thesis:

Molecular Mechanisms of Ligand-Membrane Protein Interactions. Biophysical study of a G-protein coupled receptor, VPAC1, and the peripheral benzodiazepine receptor.

Supported February 14, 2008. Under the direction of Dr. Jean-Michel Neumann, CEA

The overall goal of my PhD work has been to help elucidate the molecular mechanisms that govern protein-ligand interactions in the membrane. My thesis consists of two separate studies, the study of the peripheral benzodiazepine receptor and its interaction with cholesterol, and the study of the G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) interaction class B VPAC and its VIP peptide ligand. To do this two main techniques were used: NMR and molecular modeling.
